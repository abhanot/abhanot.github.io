Get Ready: Tropical Diseases Are Coming to Canada

Environmental weather patterns are changing across the world, and modern medicine must prepare to deal with the associated repercussions. In accordance with their 138-year record of global land and ocean surface temperatures, a 2017 report from the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration identified 2016 as the hottest year-to-date—with 2015 and 2017 ranking second and third, respectively. As a direct result of human pollution, climate change is a very real problem, with very real consequences. Global weather patterns show that winters are continuously getting warmer all over the world, and while global warming has its obvious consequences, rising sea levels and starving polar bears are not the only things to be concerned about. A 2005 study revealed that with milder winters comes an increase in the rate of year-round survival for species such as ticks and mosquitoes—including those carrying infectious diseases.

Chances are, you’ve never met anyone with Lyme disease; but that may be about to change. According to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, whose data are derived from the U.S. Center for Disease Control and Prevention, the incidence of Lyme disease in the U.S. approximately doubled from 1991 to 2014. In terms of the risk facing Canadians, the results of a study published in May 2017 suggest that even with the Paris Agreement in place, climate change is expected to significantly affect future Lyme disease risk in Canada. The Canadian federal government reports that Lyme disease incidence in Canada nearly septupled between the years of 2009 and 2016. The tick population is growing, and the number of people who’ve been infected with Lyme disease is especially high in Ontario, Nova Scotia, and Quebec.

Lyme disease is spread through ticks, but not all ticks carry Lyme disease. The Government of Canada warns that ticks can be found in forests, tall grass, and wooded areas. Those who golf, hunt, camp, fish, or even hike are especially at risk of contracting the disease. While Lyme disease is becoming more prevalent, mosquito populations are also thriving in response to climate change, increasing the spread of diseases such as malaria and dengue fever. Studies predict that by the year 2050, distribution patterns for Aedes aegypti, the yellow fever mosquito, and Aedes albopictus, the Asian tiger mosquito, will extend into much of the U.S. and parts of Western and Southeastern Canada. Among other pathogens, these mosquitoes are capable of transmitting the Zika virus, chikungunya fever, yellow fever, and dengue fever. Both species have already been captured in Canada.

Beyond ticks or mosquitoes, many species of harmful bacteria also thrive in warm climates. A 2016 study found that with increasing sea surface temperatures comes an increase in the abundance of bacteria belonging to the Vibrio genus. Many species of Vibrio are human and animal pathogens, known to give rise to cholera, infectious diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal pain, necrotizing wound infections, and sepsis. Vibrio-related illnesses are becoming more prevalent around the globe, and if you’re not worried about getting sick, yourself, you should be concerned about coastal marine life, for which Vibrio infections have been associated with climate-linked mass mortality events.

Climate change has numerous implications for human health, including its effects on the prevalence of vector-borne and waterborne diseases around the world. Software is often used to examine how agents in a population might adapt to environmental change. COBWEB—Complexity and Organized Behaviour Within Environmental Bounds—is a software package that simulates genetic change, ecological change, vector-borne disease distribution, heatwaves, and other aspects of variability in a population. This program may be used to predict how existing populations would adapt to changing environmental factors, and perhaps to devise a strategy for prevention and protection.

Further action must be taken to prevent the proliferation of tick and mosquito populations in order to protect individuals from vector-borne illness. As climate change progresses, estimated changes in temperature, humidity, precipitation, and factors of the like may make Canada a suitable environment for the growth and reproduction of many pathogens that would not have thrived in Canadian climates from earlier decades. It is likely that in our lifetimes, Canada may see outbreaks of infectious diseases that we currently consider to be “tropical”. We’ve experienced a near sevenfold increase in nationwide Lyme disease incidence over a seven-year period, and infections such as the Zika virus and dengue fever are already becoming a concern for Canadians. Climate change is a very real problem, and we’re only just beginning to encounter its consequences.
