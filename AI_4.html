Could your next favorite artist be a computer?

AI has already changed your life in many ways. When you scroll through Facebook, an algorithm decides what you see. When you read the news, many of the shorter articles may have been written by computers. The vegetables you eat might have been planted at a specific time, with the help of information generated through AI. Art, however, has yet to be affected by AI—but this might change.


Recently, an AI-produced portrait of Edmond de Belamy sold for over $400,000 at an auction in New York City. The level of interest, and the price of the painting, have ignited conversations about the future of AI in art.


When an AI paints a painting, it does so differently than a human would. The AI painter uses a machine-learning algorithm known as a generative adversarial network, or GAN. A GAN relies on two separate neural networks: a generator and a discriminator. Both parts of the system are given a set of training data—many images of a certain type of painting—that can be analyzed for patterns. For example, in the case of the portrait of Edmond de Belamy, the training data provided to the GAN would’ve consisted of many 19th-century portraits.


The GAN works by determining certain features that are common across the dataset. One such feature, at a high level, may be that each portrait must have a nose. After determining these features, the generator uses them to attempt to create new images. Each of those images is reviewed by the discriminator, which evaluates the image’s similarity against the original training data. Each time the generator’s image gets rejected by the discriminator, the GAN “learns” by incrementally tweaking the ruleset. When the generator finally “fools” the discriminator with a generated image, the AI has successfully made a new painting.


But just because it looks like a painting, is it art? What art is, or what constitutes good art, have long been subjects of debate. Is art solely that which possesses aesthetic value, or does art need to have meaning as well? Perhaps it’s entirely subjective and art is whatever each individual determines it to be; maybe there is no universal standard.


If the only criterion for being art is having aesthetic value, then AI can create art—and good art, at that. But if the piece must have a meaning or message, whether or not it constitutes art is less clear. The algorithm is not thinking about the purpose behind its creation; its only goal is to produce an image that looks as similar to its training data as possible. By this definition, the algorithm cannot make art.


AI-generated art does, however, have a human component, which could be the true source of its meaning; the art with which the human decides to train the AI model could be the root of the purpose of the piece. This line of thinking is similar to the style of Andy Warhol, who came up with the ideas for many of his pieces but left much of the actual creation to other artists.


It is unlikely that AI has the capacity to threaten individual artists or the art industry as a whole. The subjective nature of art will always leave plenty of room for human-produced art; however, AI-produced art may ultimately open the door to art creation for those who have ideas but lack the skills to bring them to fruition.
